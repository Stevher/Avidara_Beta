The Package Insert and the Patient Information Leaflet are often treated as a pair — produced together, submitted together, and updated together. That familiarity breeds a particular kind of carelessness. Teams that understand one document assume they understand the other. SAHPRA's reviewers see the consequences of that assumption regularly.

The PI and PIL are not the same document in different formats. They are different documents, governed by different requirements, written for different audiences, and reviewed against different standards. Getting either wrong delays registration, and getting both wrong at once — which is common, because errors tend to cluster — compounds the timeline significantly.

## What each document is for

The Package Insert (PI) is the primary reference document for healthcare professionals. It must contain the complete approved clinical and safety information for the product: pharmacology, pharmacokinetics, indications, contraindications, warnings, precautions, interactions, dosing, and adverse events. The PI is the anchor against which all promotional material, labelling, and downstream documents are checked. It is also the document SAHPRA reviews most carefully during registration.

The Patient Information Leaflet (PIL) is intended for the patient. It must translate the clinical content of the PI into plain language that a lay person can act on: what the medicine is for, how to take it, what to watch out for, and what to do if something goes wrong. The PIL must be accurate, comprehensible, and consistent with the PI — but it must not be a simplified copy of it. The structure, language, and reading level requirements are distinct.

## Where the PI gets rejected

**Claims outside the registration.** Every indication on the PI must match the registered indication exactly. If the product was registered for "moderate to severe hypertension in adults" and the PI says "hypertension in adults", that is a deficiency. If it says "hypertension", that is a broader claim than registered. Reviewers check this line by line.

**Outdated safety information.** SAHPRA expects the PI to reflect the current approved safety profile. Post-market safety updates — new contraindications, updated interaction warnings, revised adverse event frequencies — that were implemented in the originator's PI but not carried through to the South African PI are a common source of deficiencies, particularly for products with long registration histories.

**Missing mandatory sections.** The Regulations specify the structure of the PI. Sections that are missing entirely, merged with others without authorisation, or reordered from the approved format trigger automatic deficiencies. The scheduling information, the registration number, and the responsible person details must appear in precisely the locations required.

**Incorrect storage statements.** "Store in a cool, dry place" is not an approved storage statement. Temperature must be expressed as a numeric range. If the registered storage condition is "store below 25°C, protect from light and moisture", all three elements must appear, not a subset.

## Where the PIL gets rejected

**Plain language failures.** The PIL must be written at a reading level accessible to a patient with no medical training. Technical terms that appear in the PI must either be avoided or explained. A PIL that reads like a simplified PI — using terms like "hepatic impairment", "contraindicated", or "concomitant administration" without explanation — fails the plain language requirement regardless of accuracy.

**Inconsistency with the PI.** The PIL must not contradict the PI on any clinical point. If the PI lists five drugs with clinically significant interactions, the PIL must address all five in accessible language. If the PI warns against use in pregnancy, the PIL must carry an equivalent warning. Omissions from the PIL that are present in the PI are treated as deficiencies, not simplifications.

**Missing patient-specific instructions.** The PIL must include what to do if a dose is missed, what to do in case of overdose, how to dispose of unused medicine, and how to store the product. These are mandatory regardless of dosage form. They are also the sections most often missing when a PIL is drafted by adapting a foreign-market PIL not written to South African requirements.

**Inconsistent product name presentation.** The proprietary name and INN must appear on the PIL. The INN must be displayed with at least equal prominence to the proprietary name — the same rule that applies to the label applies here. If the PIL uses the brand name throughout and mentions the INN once in small type, it does not meet the requirement.

## The most common paired failure

The error that appears most often across both documents is the storage condition mismatch. The PI states one storage requirement, the PIL states another, and the label states a third. Each may have been drafted at different times by different people working from different source documents. The conflict is only caught when all three are reviewed together against the same reference.

Structured review that cross-references the PI, PIL, and label simultaneously — rather than reviewing each document in isolation — is the most effective way to catch this class of error before it reaches SAHPRA.
