Every promotional piece — from a detail aid to a social media post — must pass through a Medical, Legal, and Regulatory (MLR) review before it reaches a healthcare professional or consumer. Yet for many regulatory affairs teams, the MLR process remains a source of frustration: slow turnaround times, unclear feedback loops, and last-minute rejections that could have been avoided weeks earlier. Understanding what the process demands and how to structure it properly transforms MLR from a bottleneck into a quality gate that protects both patients and commercial timelines.

## What MLR Review Actually Is

MLR review is a formal, cross-functional evaluation of promotional and medical materials to ensure they are accurate, balanced, not misleading, and compliant with applicable legislation and codes. In South Africa, the governing framework includes the Medicines and Related Substances Act (Act 101 of 1965), SAHPRA's advertising and labelling guidelines, and the Marketing Code of the Pharmaceutical Industry. For multinational companies, local MLR must also align with global standard operating procedures that often reflect ICH, FDA, and EMA expectations.

The review is not a rubber stamp. Each discipline in the MLR triad examines the material through a distinct lens: Medical verifies clinical accuracy and fair balance, Legal assesses risk exposure and intellectual property considerations, and Regulatory confirms alignment with approved indications, prescribing information, and scheduling requirements. When any one of these perspectives is missing or underweight, the risk of non-compliant material reaching the market rises sharply.

## Who Should Be in the Room

Getting the right people around the table — or on the same platform — is half the battle. A functional MLR committee typically includes a medical adviser or medical information pharmacist, a regulatory affairs specialist familiar with the product's approved labelling, and legal counsel with experience in advertising law and industry codes. Depending on the material, brand managers or marketing leads attend to provide context and receive feedback directly, which reduces the back-and-forth that eats up timelines.

Importantly, the regulatory representative must have ready access to the latest approved professional information (PI), patient information leaflet (PIL), and any conditions of registration. Without these reference documents at hand, the review becomes speculative rather than evidence-based.

## How to Structure the Review File

A well-assembled submission file prevents the single most common cause of MLR delays: incomplete documentation. Each piece of material submitted for review should be accompanied by the following:

The **draft material** itself, in a format that clearly shows all text, visual elements, and references. A **reference pack** containing every clinical paper, data source, or guideline cited, with relevant passages highlighted — reviewers should not have to hunt through a 30-page study to find the claim being supported. The **current approved PI and PIL**, so that claims can be checked against registered indications and approved wording. A **claims matrix** mapping each promotional claim to its supporting reference and indicating whether the claim is direct, indirect, or implied. Finally, a **version history and prior review comments** if the material has been through an earlier round, showing how previous feedback was addressed.

This structure is not bureaucratic overhead. It is the documentation trail that demonstrates due diligence if SAHPRA, the Advertising Regulatory Board, or a competitor ever challenges the material.

## Common Bottlenecks and How to Avoid Them

**Late involvement of regulatory.** When RA teams first see a piece of material the day before a campaign launch, meaningful review becomes impossible. Regulatory should be consulted during the briefing stage, before concepts are developed, so that scheduling restrictions and claim boundaries are understood from the outset.

**Ambiguous feedback.** Comments such as "this feels off" or "check the reference" without specificity create multiple review cycles. Each reviewer should state what is wrong, cite the applicable rule or reference, and propose a resolution or acceptable alternative.

**Outdated reference documents.** If the PI was updated three months ago but the MLR file still contains the previous version, claims may be assessed against superseded wording. Maintaining a single, controlled source of current registration documents eliminates this risk.

**No clear decision authority.** When disagreements arise between Medical and Marketing — and they will — someone must have the authority to make a final call. Typically this sits with the most senior medical or regulatory representative, but the governance must be defined in an SOP, not negotiated ad hoc during each review.

### Keeping It Moving

Setting fixed MLR meeting cadences, whether weekly or fortnightly, creates predictability for all stakeholders. Materials submitted after the cut-off date roll to the next session — no exceptions. This simple discipline discourages last-minute submissions and gives reviewers adequate preparation time.

## Making It Sustainable

A robust MLR process is ultimately a documentation exercise as much as a scientific one. The quality of the review depends on the quality of the underlying regulatory documents — PIs, PILs, conditions of registration — being current, accessible, and correctly interpreted.
